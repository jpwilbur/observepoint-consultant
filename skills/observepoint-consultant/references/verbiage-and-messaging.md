# Verbiage & messaging

How to talk about ObservePoint the way ObservePoint talks about itself. Load this when the user is writing content that will be seen externally — case studies, pitch decks, customer-facing emails, internal positioning docs — or when you're framing a recommendation back to the user.

## The category

ObservePoint is a **web governance platform**. Use that phrasing.

Avoid:

- "Tag manager" — they don't manage tags, they govern what manages them.
- "Cookie scanner" — far too narrow.
- "Analytics QA tool" — accurate but undersells the privacy and accessibility scope.

The crisper short phrasing: "the web governance platform" or "automated web governance."

## The core message

Their public framing centers on bringing **insights, automation, and compliance to the chaos of your website.** The "chaos" framing is theirs and it's load-bearing — it acknowledges the customer's reality (lots of teams, lots of vendors, lots of unknowns).

Three things ObservePoint says it helps customers do:

1. **Catch hidden privacy violations** before regulators do.
2. **Reduce ad spend wasted** on broken tracking.
3. **Simplify compliance** through automation rather than manual audits.

When you summarize ObservePoint to a customer, leading with one of those three is safer than improvising.

## Capitalization and product naming

Match these exactly when writing:

- **ObservePoint** — one word, both halves capitalized. Not "Observe Point" or "Observepoint."
- **Web Audits** — capitalized.
- **Journeys** — capitalized.
- **Page Insights** — capitalized.
- **Tag & Cookie Debugger** — full name with the ampersand.
- **HAR Analyzer** — capital H, A, R, A.
- **LiveConnect** — one word, capital L and C.
- **Tag & Variable Rules** — full name; "Rules" alone is OK after introduction.
- **Touchpoints**, **JourneyStream**, **Prism** — the Strala-acquired products.

Do not pluralize product names oddly: "running an Audit" is fine; "running an Audits" is not. "Setting up Journeys" works because the unit name is the plural; "setting up a Journey" works for one.

## Customer language we use

When describing the customer's environment, prefer their words:

- "Your team" — analytics team, marketing ops, privacy team. Specific is better than generic.
- "Your stack" — the tools they already use.
- "Your domain" — their site, plural if multi-site.
- "Releases" — what they ship to production. Aligns with engineering vocabulary.
- "Releases that change tagging" — the moment where governance fails most often.

Avoid:

- "Your tag mess" — accurate sometimes, but condescending.
- "Manual processes" — they've usually invested in those; framing them as broken doesn't help.
- "We can do that too" — competitive language doesn't belong in customer conversations.

## Phrases we use and don't use

| Use this | Not this |
|---|---|
| Web governance | Tag management |
| Insights, automation, and compliance | Just analytics QA |
| The chaos of your website | The problem with your website |
| Validates what your CMP claims to do | Replaces your CMP |
| Detective control + your preventive controls | We block tags |
| Evidence pack | Proof |
| Rules engine | Assertion library |
| Catch issues before regulators do | Catch issues before lawsuits |
| Your data layer | Your dataLayer object |
| Scheduled audits | Crawls |
| Synthetic browser | Robot / bot |

## How to frame outcomes

Outcome statements work better than feature statements. Customer-facing language:

- **Less effective:** "ObservePoint has a Rules engine."
- **More effective:** "Catch a broken purchase event the moment a release ships, not three weeks later when revenue reports look wrong."

- **Less effective:** "We support CCPA."
- **More effective:** "Prove your opt-out works on every device and every page — the failure mode the regulator focuses on."

- **Less effective:** "We integrate with OneTrust."
- **More effective:** "Validate that OneTrust is actually blocking what your privacy team configured it to block."

## How to frame limitations

Honesty earns trust. Frame what ObservePoint can't do as a routing decision, not an apology:

- "ObservePoint is web-only. For native iOS and Android apps, you'd pair this with a mobile testing tool — same audit philosophy, different vendor."
- "We observe what your CMP exposes to the browser. We can prove the outcome end-to-end; we can't reach into the CMP's backend rules engine. That's by design."
- "Audits run on a synthetic browser. For real-user telemetry, Page Insights is the companion — and a real analytics platform is the source of truth for traffic numbers."

## Disclaimers we always include

When producing external content for the user — case studies, blog drafts, pitch decks:

- Customers (real ones) are referenced by name only with explicit permission. Default to "a Fortune 500 retailer" or "a large healthcare provider" when uncertain.
- Pricing claims are absent. Refer to "contact sales" or "request a demo."
- Regulatory claims are scoped to "evidence and validation" — never "achieves compliance." Compliance is decided by lawyers and regulators, not by tools.
